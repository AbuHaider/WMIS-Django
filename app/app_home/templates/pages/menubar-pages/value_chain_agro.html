Channel-I

First channel stands as the simplest and shortest chain where homestead and small-scale farmers sell directly to the consumers. Farmers can gain considerable profit (more than 100%) if they can reach the nearest favorable market. Even though this chain appears to be the most efficient one, yet their low production and the poor transportation system often discourage farmers to catch high value markets. As a result, traders grab the advantage of this condition.

Channel-II

Looking at the second channel illustrates five actors involved in the chain. As usual, farmers stand at the very beginning and can gain only 50% profit from this chain. The outreach of the market is able to catch the regional cities like CHT bazars (Ghagra, Matiranga, Guimara, Kutukchari), Chattogram (Folmondi and Firingi Bazar), Feni and Cumilla. The whole intermediate system is dominated by this channel as 60% of mangos reach these markets. It seems that faria, bepari and aratdars extract from 15% to 18% revenues in every kg.

Channel-III

Illustration of the third channel makes it evident that farmers’ contribution added values about 44% (35 BDT/kg) and bepari made 25% (20 BDT/kg) whereas the retailers’ contribution accounted for 21% (17 BDT/kg) and the rest of 10% comes from wholesalers. Most of value addition stems from the production, post-harvesting activities, and packaging which are usually performed by the farmers and beparis. Other intermediate actors rarely contribute in the process except adjusting for the transportation costs. However, most striking feature of this channel is that it reaches the national market where per kg mango is sole for 80 BDT, the highest price among three channels. Yet farmers do not enjoy any benefits from this high price rather it goes to the pockets of retailers.
